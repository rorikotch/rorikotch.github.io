CHEESE PRODUCTION & ITS EFFECTS ON THE ENVIRONMENT

America loves cheese. In fact, in 2009, McDonald’s bought 213 million pounds of it. There’s national holidays dedicated to cheese (national grilled cheese day was on April 17th, but there’s still time to prep for national cheese lover’s day, on Jan. 20). There’s blogs, social media pages and trends even dedicated to our favorite milk byproduct. On Instagram alone, there are more than 5,600 photos hashtagged with “#cheesepull.”

One thing is certain– we make a lot of cheese. In 2015, the U.S. produced 22,802,766,000 pounds. And we tend eat a lot of it too: The average American eats 630 pounds of dairy products a year, 34.1 pounds of cheese specifically. But with 321 million people in the U.S., that means only about half of all cheese produced in 2015 was consumed that year. While wasting cheese may not seem like a big deal, cheese production is pretty resource intensive. Taking care of animals, processing the milk and transportation of the finished product are just parts of the process. And as it turns out, cheese production is a contributor to climate change, or, at least the acceleration of it.

However, to understand why cheese making is taking a toll on the environment, we need to understand how it’s produced. The United States Department of Agriculture’s National Agricultural Statistics Service keeps detailed datasets on tons of subjects– including agriculture. According to Donnie Fike, a dairy products statistician in the livestock branch at the U.S. Department of Agriculture’s National Agricultural Statistics Service, the USDA uses monthly electronic forms and mail outs to collect the data for their reports. There are 1,272 dairies in the U.S., and they hear from all of them.

“It’s a monthly survey that dairy processing plants complete,” Fike said. “We get it a couple different ways– we have electronic data reporting, which is actually a website where the plant manager or bookkeeper can log in and actually put their information in. Or there are some that we actually mail out and people complete it and mail it back.”

However, some of the smaller dairies are a bit late with their reporting. According to the Dairy Products 2016 Summary, only some of the smaller dairy production facilities are contacted monthly. The others send their reports after the close of the year. However, these facilities do not make up a large part of the production totals anyway.

Cheese production all starts with milk. According to the USDA, in Feb. 2017, the U.S. produced 15.7 billion pounds of milk.

As previously stated, in 2011, the U.S. produces upwards of 22 billion pounds of cheese per year and only consumed around half of it. However, the U.S. alone is sitting on a stockpile of around 1.191 billion pounds of American cheese. Due to this excess amount of cheese, the USDA announced that it would buy $20 million dollars’ worth of cheese and donate it to local food banks. In their April 24 to April 28, 2017 report the United States Department of Agriculture’s Dairy Market News the report states that milk is in good supply, and is increasing, which means cheese production will continue to rise as well. However, the underlying question remains– what the heck is the U.S. doing with all that leftover cheese?!

According to the U.S. Dairy Export Council, from 2015 to 2016 U.S. exports of cheese rose 8 percent, but exports have been rising since around Dec. 2010.

According to the U.S. Dairy Export Council, from 2015 to 2016 U.S. exports of cheese rose 8 percent. However, exports have been rising since around Dec. 2010.

The largest importers of U.S. cheese include Mexico, South East Asia, Pakistan, China and Peru, respectively. In 2016, the U.S. exported a total of 528,606 pounds of cheese just to these countries alone– and all of this transportation adds up. According to the U.S. Dairy Export Council’s Global Dairy Market Outlook, exportation of U.S. cheese has increased by eight percent from 2016.

While the U.S. is a large exporter for cheese, the demand for imported cheese has increased drastically throughout the recent years. In 2012, 11,277,329 pounds of cheddar cheese, alone was sold in the U.S. This amount of cheese is about equivalent to 1,128 conventional school buses. According to the USDA’s Dairy Outlook from Feb. 26, 2016 the exports of cheese dropped by 14 percent from 2014 to 2015. However, the U.S. imports of cheese was 23 percent higher in 2015 as compared to 2014.
